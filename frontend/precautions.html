Flood Precaution

1. Before the Flood

1.1 Home Preparedness

Strengthening household infrastructure reduces water damage and improves safety.

  • Elevate electrical appliances and seal wall cracks.
  • Store valuables and documents in waterproof containers.
  • Keep sandbags or bricks ready to block water entry.

1.2 Emergency Kit Preparation

A well-stocked emergency kit ensures survival and comfort during displacement.

  • Include dry food, clean water, flashlight, batteries, first aid, and essential medicines.
  • Add copies of ID cards, land papers, and insurance documents.

1.3 Awareness & Planning

Knowing your environment and evacuation routes reduces panic and confusion.

  • Identify nearby shelters, hospitals, and high ground.
  • Share evacuation plans with family and neighbors.
  • Subscribe to local alert systems (SMS, WhatsApp, radio).

2. During the Flood

2.1 Safe Evacuation

Timely evacuation prevents injury and loss of life.

  • Leave early if advised by authorities.
  • Assist elderly, children, and disabled individuals.
  • Carry essentials and avoid returning until declared safe.

2.2 Avoiding Hazards

Floodwaters conceal dangers that can cause injury or death.

  • Never walk or drive through moving water.
  • Stay away from electric poles, transformers, and submerged wires.
  • Avoid contaminated water sources.

2.3 Shelter Behavior

Cooperation in shelters ensures safety and resource sharing.

  • Bring your own bedding and basic supplies.
  • Follow instructions from volunteers and officials.
  • Maintain hygiene and help others when possible.

3. After the Flood

3.1 Home Recovery

Cleaning and disinfecting prevents disease and restores living conditions.

  • Use bleaching powder or disinfectants to clean surfaces.
  • Dispose of spoiled food and sanitize utensils.
  • Check for structural damage before reoccupying.

3.2 Water & Food Safety

Contaminated resources can lead to outbreaks of illness.

  • Boil drinking water or use purification tablets.
  • Avoid using wells or hand pumps until cleared by officials.
  • Consume only packaged or verified food supplies.

3.3 Mental & Social Support

Emotional recovery is essential for long-term resilience.

  • Talk to neighbors and share experiences.
  • Seek counseling if feeling anxious or overwhelmed.
  • Participate in community rebuilding efforts.

4. Community-Level Actions

4.1 Neighborhood Watch

Local coordination improves response speed and resource sharing.

  • Form groups to monitor water levels and coordinate evacuations.
  • Share boats, tractors, and first aid kits.

4.2 Awareness Drives

Education empowers people to act safely and responsibly.

  • Organize street plays, posters, or school sessions on flood safety.
  • Encourage youth to volunteer during emergencies.

Crop Disaster Precaution

1. Before the Disaster: Planning & Risk Reduction

1.1 Crop Selection & Diversification

Diversifying crops reduces dependency and spreads risk across seasons.

  • Choose flood-tolerant or short-duration varieties (e.g., jute, maize, moong).
  • Avoid sowing in low-lying or flood-prone plots during monsoon.
  • Use staggered planting schedules to minimize total loss.

1.2 Field Preparation & Drainage

Proper land management improves water flow and reduces crop damage.

  • Build raised bunds and contour trenches to redirect water.
  • Install field drains to prevent waterlogging.
  • Add organic matter to strengthen soil structure.

1.3 Personal Documentation & Insurance

Having records and coverage ensures financial recovery.

  • Enroll in crop insurance schemes like PMFBY.
  • Keep land records, sowing dates, and crop photos safe and updated.
  • Store documents in waterproof bags or digital backups.

2. During the Disaster: Emergency Crop Protection

2.1 Protecting Standing Crops

Timely action can reduce irreversible damage.

  • Drain stagnant water from fields within 48 hours.
  • Use sandbags or temporary bunds to protect nursery beds.
  • Apply foliar sprays to revive stressed plants.

2.2 Safeguarding Farm Inputs & Livelihood

Preserving farm assets ensures continuity after the flood.

  • Move seeds, fertilizers, and tools to elevated storage.
  • Relocate livestock to safe shelters with feed and water.
  • Avoid pesticide use during floods to prevent runoff contamination.

3. After the Disaster: Recovery & Replanting

3.1 Soil Health Restoration

Rebuilding soil fertility is essential for replanting.

  • Test soil for pH and nutrient loss.
  • Apply gypsum or lime to neutralize acidity.
  • Use green manure or legumes to restore organic content.

3.2 Replanting & Income Recovery

Short-duration crops offer quick returns and food security.

  • Choose fast-growing varieties like urad, moong, or maize.
  • Use direct seeding or transplanting based on field condition.
  • Coordinate with local agriculture officers for seed support.

3.3 Claim Filing & Financial Aid

Timely documentation helps secure compensation.

  • Submit crop loss reports within the official window.
  • Attach geo-tagged photos and field survey forms.
  • Follow up with agriculture offices or Common Service Centres (CSCs).

4. Community-Level Civilian Actions

4.1 Farmer Networks & Resource Sharing

Collective action improves resilience and access to support.

  • Form local groups to share tools, seeds, and transport.
  • Organize community seed banks and emergency storage hubs.

4.2 Awareness & Training

Educated farmers make faster, safer decisions.

  • Attend workshops on climate-smart agriculture and flood safety.
  • Promote youth involvement in farm safety and disaster response.
